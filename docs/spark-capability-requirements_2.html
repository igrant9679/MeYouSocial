01 · Executive Summary

A content engine that sounds like LSI Media — at scale

Spark turns the agency's manual, interview-driven article process into a repeatable system that produces highly personalized, expert-level content — never generic AI filler — and carries it all the way from idea to published post to social distribution.

Today, producing one strong article requires a founder to sit through a 10-question voice interview so the writing reflects real expertise. That produces excellent results but cannot be delegated or scaled. Spark solves this by capturing each subject-matter expert's knowledge once into a reusable profile, grounding every draft in the agency's real credentials, case studies, and keyword strategy, and shaping the voice through the proprietary 7 Motifs™ framework.

The platform is multi-tenant from day one: LSI Media runs as the first workspace, and the same engine can be offered to clients as a managed service, each with isolated branding, strategy, keyword data, and connected sites.

SME

Personalized, not generic

SME profiles + real credentials (DHS Trusted Tester, PMP, MBE, Adobe Partner) ground every draft in defensible expertise.

7M

Motif-driven voice

Select from the 7 Motifs to dictate tone — Visionary, Sincere, Competitive, and more — per article and per channel.

⟳

Manual → Autonomous

Run a single piece by hand, schedule a monthly cadence, or let Spark operate the full pipeline with approval gates.

Primary objectives

  • Eliminate the bottleneck — replace the per-article interview with stored SME knowledge that answers "as the expert would."
  • Protect brand authority — enforce a hard "never fabricate a statistic, study, or citation" rule; cite only verified, retrieved sources.
  • Operationalize the SEO strategy — the four-tier keyword model and page-mapping workbook become the app's living configuration, not a static file.
  • Close the loop to publishing — generate the article, the Squirrly/Rank Math/Yoast SEO fields, accessible markup, image specs, and push to WordPress.
  • Distribute, don't stop at publish — auto-derive motif-aligned social variants for LinkedIn, X, Instagram and Facebook.
  • Measure and feed back — pull GSC/GA4 performance to prioritize the next ideas and flag underperformers, while shielding posts that already rank.

Explicit non-goals (for v1)

  • Not a replacement for Google Search Console as the source of truth for rankings/indexation.
  • Not a full DAM or video editor — it briefs and slots assets, it doesn't replace creative production.
  • Not a paid-ads manager — organic content and organic social only.
  • Not an autonomous publisher without approval gates unless a workspace explicitly opts in per content type.
02 · Scope & Pipeline

Nine stages, one continuous loop

Every piece of content flows through the same pipeline. Each stage can be automated, assisted, or fully manual, and each can require human approval before the next begins.

STAGE 1

Ideate

Mine customer questions, keywords, gaps & trends into a scored idea board.

STAGE 2

Personalize

Pull the relevant SME profile, case studies & credentials for the topic.

STAGE 3

Generate

Draft in the chosen motif voice with correct heading structure.

STAGE 4

Optimize

Produce SEO fields for the selected plugin; build internal links from page mapping.

STAGE 5

Assets

Brief/generate OG + featured images at correct dimensions; alt text.

STAGE 6

Review

Anti-slop & accessibility checks; human approval gates by role.

STAGE 7

Publish

Push to WordPress with schema, canonical, accessible markup.

STAGE 8

Distribute

Motif-aligned social variants, scheduled per platform.

STAGE 9

Analyze

GSC/GA4 performance feeds the next round of ideas.

Design principle (from the brand): "Marketing first, design second, technology third." Spark enforces this order — strategy and motif drive the content; SEO and accessibility shape its structure; publishing mechanics come last.
03 · The 7 Motifs™ Tone Engine

Motif selection dictates voice & tone

LSI Media's 7 Motifs™ framework maps emotional and rational appeals into communications to "ignite the spark" between a brand and its customers. In Spark, the chosen motif(s) directly steer the writing voice, the angle, the calls-to-action, and the social copy.

01

Visionary

Thought leadership & forward-thinking ideas that position the brand as a game changer.

02

Competitive

Creates urgency by showing how the brand beats alternatives on price, benefits & features.

03

Succinct

Directness — essential information upfront so the reader never has to hunt for it.

04

Sincere

Authenticity — focused on solving real pain points rather than simply selling.

05

Exclusive

Makes the reader feel significant and special through a VIP, insider tone.

06

Social

Builds community around shared values and invites the reader to "join" a cause.

07

Informative

Positions the brand as a consultative knowledge resource that educates.

How motifs operate inside Spark

  • Multi-select with weighting — a piece can blend motifs (e.g. 70% Informative + 30% Visionary). The dominant motif sets structure; secondary motifs color the CTA and intro.
  • Per-channel motif mapping — the article may be Informative while its social variants lean Social, mirroring how the framework already shifts the "Social" motif onto social media.
  • Workspace defaults + per-idea override — each workspace sets default motifs by content tier/audience; authors override per article.
  • Prompt translation — each motif maps to a concrete, versioned style directive (voice, sentence rhythm, evidence type, CTA pattern) injected into the generation prompt — editable by admins, not hard-coded.
  • Motif A/B testing — optionally generate two motif treatments of the same idea and compare downstream engagement (see Ideas).

Multiple companies, isolated by workspace

Each company/website is a workspace with its own brand kit, motif defaults, keyword strategy, SME profiles, connected WordPress site, and social accounts. Users sign in once and switch between the workspaces they're entitled to.

05 · Functional Requirements

What the system must do

Grouped by module. Each requirement is testable; IDs are stable references for build tickets.

FR-1Authentication & Workspaces
  • Secure login with email/password + optional SSO (Google/Microsoft) and MFA.
  • A user may belong to many workspaces with different roles; a workspace switcher is always available.
  • Strict data isolation between workspaces; no cross-workspace data leakage in search, generation, or analytics.
  • Admins invite users by email and assign roles; account creation/passwords handled by the invitee.
FR-2Brand, Typography & Motif Configuration
  • Store a brand kit per workspace (colors, fonts, logo, footer credit).
  • Heading / title style spec — per workspace, define each heading level (H1–H6) with: font pixel size, top margin (px), and bottom margin (px) (and optionally font family, weight, line-height, color). These are article-scoped visual styles, set independently of the theme's site-wide heading sizes (which are often oversized for heroes), so articles read correctly while the semantic H-tag order is always preserved.
  • Editable image dimensions — per workspace, enter the pixel dimensions for the featured image (e.g. 1920×1080) and the Open Graph image (default 1200×630); both are editable fields, not fixed.
  • Design-system rendering profile — choose how content renders on the connected site: Avada/Fusion Builder elements, Gutenberg blocks, or clean semantic HTML (see FR-18).
  • Manage the 7 Motifs as editable style directives; set workspace defaults by tier & audience.
  • Allow per-article motif selection (single or weighted multi-select) and per-channel motif mapping.
FR-3SME Knowledge Profiles
  • One-time structured intake per expert (the former 10-question interview) capturing experience, expertise, opinions, case studies, examples, and "things we always/never say."
  • Support voice and text intake; version profiles over time.
  • Ingest existing sources to seed knowledge: website content, services agreement, portfolio, prior articles.
  • At generation time, select the relevant SME profile(s) so drafts answer "as the expert would."
FR-4Keyword & Strategy Workbook
  • Hold the 4-tier keyword model, master keyword list, page mapping, and blog content ideas as living data (import existing workbook).
  • Each keyword carries tier, service, audience, intent, and target page; volume/difficulty fields populated only from real research integrations — never invented.
  • On-demand keyword research and scheduled list refresh (add/swap on a set interval) covering primary & secondary phrases.
  • Maintain the internal-link graph derived from page mapping for use during optimization.
FR-5Idea Engine
  • Discover ideas from: customer-question sources (People-Also-Ask, AlsoAsked-style, community questions), the keyword workbook gaps, competitor gaps, calendar/seasonal hooks, and analytics signals.
  • Auto-tag each idea with tier, audience, target service page, suggested motif(s), and a priority score.
  • De-duplicate against existing published posts; flag refresh candidates instead of net-new where appropriate.
  • Present ideas on a board; allow manual add, edit, merge, approve, reject, and "send to draft."
FR-6Content Generation
  • Generate long-form drafts following the house template: question-reframing intro, sectioned body, "mindset shift" takeaway, CTA, and (where claims exist) a verified sources list.
  • Enforce correct semantic heading hierarchy (one H1, ordered H2/H3) independent of visual styling.
  • Apply the selected motif voice and ground content in the SME profile + workspace facts.
  • Respect track-based length targets (cornerstone 2,000+, service-supporting 1,200–1,800).
  • Hard rule: never fabricate statistics, studies, quotes, or citations. Claims requiring evidence must be backed by a retrieved, linkable source or flagged for human sourcing.
FR-7SEO Optimization
  • Produce the full SEO field set: URL slug, title (primary + fallback, ≤60 ch), meta description (≤155 ch), focus keyword, secondary keywords, canonical, OG title/description/image spec.
  • Output mapped to the workspace's chosen plugin: Squirrly, Rank Math, or Yoast (field-name translation per plugin).
  • Apply slug convention rules per content type (blog vs service page) from one canonical setting.
  • Suggest 2–3 internal links (with anchor text) from the page-mapping graph + one credible external link.
  • Generate "Notes for the publisher": focus-keyword rationale, content tier, internal-link targets, cornerstone guidance.
FR-8Asset Generation & Image Requirements
  • Every article requires two images: a featured image and a branded Open Graph image. Publishing is blocked until both exist at the workspace-defined pixel dimensions, each with alt text.
  • Produce an image brief for the OG image (workspace-set dimensions, default 1200×630, branded, anti-stock guidance) and the featured image (workspace-set dimensions, e.g. 1920×1080) — dimensions are pulled from the editable fields in FR-2.
  • The OG image is always branded (logo/lockup) for social/search previews; in-body and featured images stay clean unless the workspace opts into branding them.
  • Validate that supplied/generated images match the required pixel dimensions; warn on mismatch and offer crop/resize to spec.
  • Optional AI image generation behind a per-workspace toggle, always routed through human review (quality concern documented).
  • Generate descriptive alt text for every meaningful image; mark decorative images with empty alt.
FR-9Accessibility (WCAG 2.1 AA)
  • Validate heading order, descriptive link text (no "click here"), alt-text presence, and labeled elements before publish.
  • Flag color-contrast and structure issues; integrate with the workspace accessibility checker (e.g. Equalize Digital) where connected.
  • Block or warn on publish when AA checks fail, per workspace policy.
FR-10Review & Approval Workflow
  • Configurable multi-stage approval (draft → SEO → assets → final) with role-based gates and reviewer assignment.
  • Automated "anti-slop" pre-checks: unsupported claims, generic filler, off-brand tone, missing SME grounding, accessibility failures.
  • Inline comments, change requests, version history, and audit trail of who approved what and when.
  • Two-way status sync with Nifty (Planned → Doing/In Process → For Review → Done) via its API.
FR-11WordPress Publishing
  • Publish/schedule posts via the WordPress REST API to the workspace's connected site.
  • Apply correct semantic heading hierarchy — one H1, ordered H2/H3/H4 — and style each level using the workspace heading spec (px size + top/bottom margins from FR-2), so article headings render at article-appropriate sizes while keeping accessible, SEO-valid tag order.
  • Render with the workspace's design-system elements — checklists with checkmarks, content/callout boxes, styled quotes, alerts, FAQ toggles, buttons and separators rather than flat text (see FR-18).
  • Write SEO plugin fields, canonical, schema, and OG tags; set the featured image and branded OG image (FR-8).
  • Set categories, tags, author, and publish date; support draft-in-WP handoff.
  • Respect existing URL/redirect rules; never overwrite a higher-performing existing post without explicit confirmation.
FR-12Social Distribution (via Uniple)
  • Auto-derive platform-specific variants (LinkedIn, X, Instagram, Facebook) from the published article, in the channel's mapped motif.
  • Respect per-platform length, hashtag, and link conventions; attach the correct image size per network.
  • Publish & schedule through Uniple — Spark owns variant generation, motif mapping, per-platform formatting, and approval; Uniple is the integration layer that handles the actual scheduling and posting to each network and manages the per-network account connections/tokens. Spark holds a single Uniple connection per workspace instead of four separate network integrations.
  • On approval, push the scheduled variants to Uniple; reflect post status back into the workflow (→ Distributed) and, where Uniple exposes it, pull basic engagement back into analytics.
  • Fallback: queue for manual posting or export if a network/asset type isn't supported by Uniple.
To confirm: Uniple's exact supported networks, scheduling API, media/asset constraints, and whether it returns post status & engagement — these drive the connector spec and shouldn't be assumed.
FR-13Automation & Scheduling
  • Three run modes per content type: Manual, Scheduled, and Autonomous.
  • Editorial calendar with cadence rules (e.g. one cornerstone + two supporting per month).
  • Guardrails: spend caps, max auto-publishes per period, mandatory human gate toggles, and a global pause/kill switch.
FR-14Analytics & Feedback Loop
  • Connect GSC & GA4; show clicks, impressions, position, organic sessions, and conversions per post.
  • Track rankings for priority keywords; surface movers and underperformers.
  • Feed performance back into the Idea Engine to prioritize topics and identify refresh candidates.
  • Flag and protect top-performing existing posts from disruptive rewrites.
FR-15Existing-Content Audit
  • Crawl the connected site and cross-reference GSC to inventory existing posts by performance.
  • Detect low-quality / "AI slop" content and propose rewrite, merge, or retire — never auto-deleting.
  • Preserve URLs and ranking history; recommend redirects rather than breaking links.
FR-16Notifications & Integrations
  • Configurable notifications (in-app, email, Slack) for approvals, publishes, failures, and schedule events.
  • Nifty task creation/updates throughout the workflow; GSC/GA4, WordPress, social, and keyword-research connectors.
FR-17YouTube & Competitor IntelligenceIdea source
  • Trend & topic research — surface demand signals from YouTube: trending and fast-rising videos in the workspace's niche, video titles/tags/descriptions, YouTube search autocomplete, and "suggested/also-watched" clusters, all mined into draftable blog topics.
  • Question mining from comments — extract recurring questions and pain points from comments on high-performing videos in the niche, as aggregate themes (no harvesting or profiling of individual commenters).
  • Competitor channel tracking — register competitor channels and monitor their most-viewed and most-recent videos, upload cadence, and topic mix to read what themes they're investing in.
  • Gap analysis — flag topics competitors cover (or that are trending) where the workspace has no equivalent content, scored as content opportunities.
  • Velocity signal — contribute a "trend velocity" factor to the idea score, treated as a leading indicator that must be validated against real search data before commitment (never invented).
  • Cross-purpose output — because the workspace may also produce video, YouTube-sourced ideas can be tagged for blog, video, or both, and flow into the repurposing pipeline (article ↔ video).
  • Compliance — use the official YouTube Data API within quota; no scraping that violates platform terms; deeper historical/competitor metrics may require an approved third-party provider (flagged, not faked).
FR-18Design-System Rendering ProfileHigh value
  • Published articles must look hand-built in the site's design system — not pasted as flat text. Spark maps content patterns in the draft to native theme components.
  • Avada / Fusion Builder element library (when the workspace uses Avada): render lists of benefits/steps as checklists with checkmark icons; tips/notes/callouts as content boxes or alerts; key statements as styled quotes / pullquotes; FAQ sections as toggles (accordions); CTAs as Avada buttons; and section breaks as separators.
  • Configurable mapping rules per workspace — which content pattern maps to which element, with sensible defaults, editable by an admin.
  • Theme abstraction — the same article renders as Fusion elements for an Avada site, Gutenberg blocks for a block-theme site, or clean semantic HTML elsewhere, selected by the workspace's rendering profile (FR-2).
  • Accessibility preserved — design elements never override semantic structure: checkmark lists remain real lists, toggles remain keyboard-operable, and heading order stays valid regardless of visual styling.
  • Apply the workspace heading style spec (px sizes + top/bottom margins) to the rendered output so typography matches the brand within the article context.

07 · Workflow & Approvals

Manual, scheduled, or autonomous — same states, different triggers

Content lifecycle states

Idea→ Approved Idea→ Drafting→ Draft Review→ SEO & A11y Review→ Assets Pending→ Final Approval→ Scheduled→ Published→ Distributed→ Analyzing

Each transition maps to a Nifty status (Planned → Doing/In Process → For Review → Done) and emits notifications. Any gate can be set to auto-pass or require a named role.

The three run modes

▶ Manual
Human-driven, step by step. An author picks an idea, triggers generation, edits, optimizes, and clicks through each gate. Spark assists; the human decides every transition. Best for high-stakes flagship pieces.
◷ Scheduled
Cadence-driven. The calendar fires jobs (e.g. "draft next cornerstone on the 1st"). Spark advances the piece to the first human gate and waits. Best for a reliable monthly rhythm with oversight.
⚡ Autonomous
Goal-driven, within guardrails. Spark ideates, drafts, optimizes, briefs assets, and either parks at Final Approval or — if a workspace opts in per content type — publishes and distributes. Spend caps and a kill switch always apply.

Approval workflow rules

  • Role-gated transitions — e.g. Editors can move to Draft Review; only Strategist/Owner (or assigned Client Reviewer) can grant Final Approval.
  • Automated pre-gates — anti-slop, citation-verification, and accessibility checks must pass (or be explicitly overridden with a logged reason) before a human gate appears.
  • Parallel review — SEO and asset review can run concurrently to compress cycle time.
  • Escalation & SLA — items idle past a threshold notify the next approver; nothing publishes by silence in supervised modes.
  • Full audit trail — every state change, approval, override, and AI action is logged per workspace.

09 · Ideas & Enhancements

Ideas functionality & future-facing concepts

Idea generation — sources & scoring

  • Question mining — People-Also-Ask, AlsoAsked-style question trees, and community questions surfaced as draftable topics.
  • Strategy gaps — keywords in the workbook with no targeting page yet; tiers under-served vs the 50/25/15/10 audience weighting.
  • Competitor & SERP gaps — topics competitors rank for that the workspace doesn't.
  • YouTube trend & competitor intel — trending/fast-rising videos, search autocomplete, and competitor-channel topic mix in the niche, plus question themes mined from video comments (see FR-17).
  • Seasonal & calendar hooks — recurring monthly content (the existing social calendar cadence) and dated opportunities.
  • Performance-driven — analytics flags refresh candidates and "expand this cluster" opportunities.
  • Idea score — composite of intent, tier fit, audience priority, difficulty (real data only), internal-link leverage, and a YouTube trend-velocity signal (validated, never invented).

YouTube research & competitor intelligence

YouTube is the second-largest search engine, which makes it a strong leading indicator of topic demand — often surfacing interest before it peaks in Google. Spark treats it as both an idea source and a competitor-watch channel.

▶

Trending topics

Fast-rising videos, search autocomplete, titles/tags, and "also-watched" clusters in the niche become draftable blog topics with a trend-velocity score.

?

Question themes

Recurring questions and pain points mined from comments on popular videos — as aggregate themes, never individual profiling — feed the same question pool as PAA/AlsoAsked.

⚔

Competitor watch

Track competitor channels: most-viewed and newest videos, upload cadence, and topic mix, with gap analysis flagging themes they cover that you don't.

Leading indicator, not gospel: a YouTube signal raises an idea's velocity score but must be validated against real search data before it's committed — the same "never invent numbers" discipline applied everywhere. Deep historical/competitor metrics that the official API doesn't expose may need an approved third-party provider, and are flagged as estimates rather than fabricated.
LSI-specific upside: because the agency also produces video and runs its own channel, YouTube-sourced ideas can be tagged for blog, video, or both — feeding the repurposing pipeline (one topic → article ↔ video ↔ social) rather than just the blog.

Workflow ideas

  • Content clusters / pillars — group ideas into a pillar + supporting set so internal linking and topical authority are planned, not accidental.
  • Motif A/B testing — generate two motif treatments and measure which earns more engagement per audience.
  • AI-search / GEO mode — an optimization pass that structures content for citation by AI answer engines (clear entities, extractable answers, matched structured data) — directly aligned to the agency's own thesis.
  • Repurposing pipeline — one article → social set → newsletter blurb → an FAQ entry, all motif-aware.
  • Brand-voice learning — fine-tune tone from the workspace's best existing posts to sharpen the SME profile over time.
  • Template library — reusable structures (comparison, checklist+PDF, buyer guide, case study) tied to tiers and motifs.

Approval-workflow ideas

  • Confidence-based routing — high-confidence, low-risk pieces get a lighter gate; claim-heavy or client-facing pieces get stricter review.
  • One-click client review links — a scoped link lets a client approve/comment without a full account.
  • "Explain the changes" diffs — every AI revision shows what changed and why, so reviewers trust fast.
  • Source dossier — each draft ships with its verified citations attached for quick reviewer verification.
  • Quiet hours & rate limits — no autonomous publishing outside configured windows.
10 · Non-Functional Requirements

Quality attributes

Truthfulness (hard constraint)

No fabricated statistics, studies, quotes, or citations — ever. Evidence-bearing claims require a retrieved, linkable source or are flagged. This is enforced, logged, and non-overridable in autonomous mode.

Security & privacy

Per-workspace data isolation; encrypted, scoped, revocable OAuth tokens; least-privilege roles; no automated account creation or password handling.

Accessibility of the app

Spark itself meets WCAG 2.1 AA — it would be off-brand for an accessibility agency's tool to fail its own standard.

Auditability

Full trail of AI actions, edits, approvals, overrides, and publishes per workspace; exportable.

Reliability & recovery

Failed jobs retry and surface clearly; nothing publishes silently on error; a global kill switch halts automation instantly.

Cost control

Per-workspace AI spend caps, usage visibility, and model selection to balance quality vs cost.

Performance

Generation streams progressively; long jobs run async with status; dashboards load fast under multi-workspace load.

Extensibility

Pluggable SEO plugins, social networks, research providers, and PM tools so new integrations don't require core rewrites.
